Underground Garbage Disposal

A design and prototype for New York City's cleanliness

Agenda

Garbage overflow in NYC streets causes sidewalks to become dirty and difficult to walk through! This is compounded by the irritating odor that stems from piled-up trash due to littering and a weak drainage system.

The Underground Garbage disposal system can assist this City crisis by:

  • Storing garbage underground, giving a bigger space for deposits.
  • Allowing for rapid and seamless collection of trash and reducing the amount of trash bags used.
  • Moving the trash underground to reduce visibility of garbage in public areas.
  • Keeping the pungent odor below street level and reducing pollutants released into the air.

Objective

  • Design a garbage can whose top piece aligns with the dimensions of a garbage truck's rear.
  • Create an underground trash can that can easily be emptied into a garbage truck, making collection more efficient and eliminating street piles.

Main Issues & Data

  • The Waste Scale: New York City produces approximately 44 million pounds (about 20 million kilograms) of waste daily, with 24 million pounds collected by the sanitation department. Annually, NYC produces nearly 2.6 million tons of trash (about 5,511,556,555 pounds), with Brooklyn generating the most on average.
  • Vulnerable Systems: The city drainage system is vulnerable to rainstorms, often flooding and failing to drain fully.

Solutions

Set up organized, larger-sized trash bins and pile trash underground to occupy more space. Implementing green infrastructure is crucial. While countries like the Netherlands have similar systems, this setup is necessary for a major city like NYC. Ultimately, NYC can start building these underground systems in newly developed areas across all boroughs and gradually roll it out over the years.

Prototype Development

It is important to acknowledge the materials used for this project:

  • Cardboard (developing a miniature crane attached to the garbage can and its rear to the truck).
  • Arduino Uno, L298 Motor Driver Module, DC Gear Motor, Servo Motor, 2 Push Buttons, Mini Breadboard, Jumper Wires, Double Battery Holder, Crane structure (Cardboard/Wood/Acrylic), and a USB Cable.

Difficulties Encountered

As the program neared its end, replicating a scaled-down truck was unreasonable. Instead, I constructed a firm cardboard box wrapped with tape to represent the neck of a crane. Another issue was weight and dimensions. Additionally, a 9V battery was used instead of a 3.7V battery to supply enough power to the L298 motor driver. Wiring directly to digital pins was tedious, so I super-glued the wires to the 12V and GND entrances on the motor driver.

OnShape Design & Final Verdict

Using the structure mentioned, the smaller servo motor is controlled by the Arduino Uno to rotate the crane arm when the first button is pressed. When the second button is pressed, the larger yellow DC motor is powered by the L298 driver to reel in the string attached to the can.

The larger 3D modeled piece is not shown due to an alignment error with the crane arm; the string causes the can to swing and miss the extension. This issue stemmed from misjudged dimensions in OnShape. For future improvement, using two DC motors would be ideal because the crane arm weight is too heavy for a standard servo motor.
